Skittles Sorter

We were tasked to create hardware based on an Arduino micro-controller with an input sensor and output device. Since we cannot be bothered to manually sort our favorite flavors of Skittles we created a machine to do it for us. Now we can eat the flavor we want and satisfy our OCD. This was implemented using two servo motors, a color sensor all connected to an Arduino Mega.

Github Repository

Context

For this project we were tasked to design a solution to an everyday problem using an Arduino micro-controller connected to an input sensor and an output device. Our initial thoughts revolved around the idea of creating an alarm based device that would be triggered either through a laser tripwire, proximity sensor, supersonic radar, etc. We then landed on two ideas that we thought would be more interesting and fun then a triggered alarm; a candy sorter and a secret knock lock. We decided on the Skittles sorter because we like candy and we each one of us in our group is just a little OCD. We believed that in this project the WOW factor would require use of simple machines or motors as a simple sensor and alarm or light would be too simple. Once we agreed on a concept, we headed to work on asset creation and calibration of the color sensor. We picked up a sensor from SolarBotics and headed to work.

Rough Sketches

As stated earlier our early ideation revolved around simple alarm based devices that would be triggered through various input sensors. We strayed away from that idea as we thought it would be extremely boring or simple. The ideas that we found most interesting involved the use of a motor and simple machines to act more on the side of automation or machinery rather than a simple notification box. We landed on two ideas - the secret knock unlock and skittles sorter - and decided on the sorter since the task completed was the most interesting and we could eat it too.

Refined Sketches

To settle the age old debate on which Skittle flavor is the best we decided to carry on with this concept. To be able to use a sensor not offered in class was another big motivation as we could ensure that our project was different from the rest. Understanding how to use the Arduino had a learning curve as someone who is not versed in hardware might have an issue finding out which wires go where and what pins connected to what but after some trial and error we were able to succeed in getting the color sensor to read the RGB values. The majority of the project's difficulty actually came in making and aligning the chassis in which the motors and the sensor would be housed. We painted the feed that moved the Skittles in front of the sensor black as we found that the white light that reflected into the sensor was too prominent in the readings. Calibration of the colors was a bit difficult as well as we found that Red, Green and Orange had similar RGB values and the sensor wasn't consistent enough to narrow the values in tighter conditional statements. We did however manage to get it working with 95% accuracy.

Final Product

The final product is definitely a prototype but the concept is there and the sensor is accurate enough to sort a bag of Skittles. Further implementation for this color sorter would be to add a larger capacity driver/feeder, calibrate the sensor to deal with more colors or different candies (Smarties) and house the sensor to ensure color accuracy. Each one of us dealt with a little bit of everything where my focus was dealing with the housing the components, creation of the chassis and calibration of the sensor.
